Team & continuity

HWVC has asked directly how the Company covers the departing co-founder's role. The answer is not that the CEO absorbs it. It is that the operating model was already distributed across specialists, and the co-founder held a role rather than a capability. This tab sets out what he owned, who owns it now, and what the Company is buying with the raise to close the remaining gap.

The honest position, stated first

A single-founder company carries key-person risk, and the Company does not pretend otherwise. What follows is not a claim that nothing changed — it is the plan for what changed. Two of the four functions the co-founder held were already being performed by others before his departure. One is structurally outsourced by design and never depended on him. One — the day-to-day operating load — is the gap the raise is explicitly funding, through a marketing/PR hire at close and a dedicated operations lead once the first tournament validates run-rate.

Why this is more robust than it looks

Three structural facts, each verifiable rather than asserted:

  1. The community engine is not a founder. The leagues that run twice weekly today — 22 players across two sessions, growing — are operated by ambassadors, not by the departed co-founder. Two of the four ambassadors were recruited out of league play itself. The engine that recruits the operators is the product, and it is already running.
  2. The supply chain was never in-house. A non-exclusive roaster relationship means no single-supplier dependency and no manufacturing capability to replace. Losing a co-founder does not remove the ability to make the product, because the Company never made the product.
  3. The load that remains is a hiring problem, not a capability problem. Marketing/PR and an operations lead are both budgeted, scoped, and standard to hire. They are gated on capital, not on scarcity — which is precisely what the Initial Closing resolves.
The residual risk, named

Between the wire and the operations hire, the CEO carries a concentrated load through the first tournament. The Company's mitigations are the ambassador bench (four operators with community standing), per-event contract staffing rather than fixed headcount, and a deliberately narrow menu built for cart-speed execution. The Company would rather state this plainly than have HWVC discover it.

The distinction that matters

Why leagues are not a profit line — and why that is deliberate

A league season is priced to be accessible, not lucrative. Its purpose is to convert a casual player into a committed member of a community, at a cost per acquisition far below what paid marketing would cost — and then to keep them. It is the top of the funnel and the retention layer at once.

What the league doesWhy it pays for itself many times over
Acquires players A player who joins a league is a player who enters tournaments. The league is how a stranger becomes a customer — and it costs the Company almost nothing net to run
Recruits the operators Two of the four current ambassadors came out of league play. The league is not just a customer funnel — it is the talent funnel for the people who run the business
Creates the drink habit Recurring weekly play is where the courtside drink becomes a ritual rather than a novelty. Tournament beverage volume depends on a habit the leagues build
Proves the community thesis Recurring, organically growing play is the evidence that this is a culture and not a fad — the single claim the entire brand rests on
De-risks the tournaments A tournament with a standing league base behind it fills. A tournament without one is a cold-start marketing problem

Read the league line as a marketing budget that happens to break even — not as a revenue line that underperforms. Judged as a profit centre it looks weak; judged as customer acquisition it is extraordinarily cheap.

Where the money is actually made

Tournaments carry the economics, and they do so on two revenue lines that scale differently:

Revenue lineHow it scalesWhy it matters
Entry fees Linear with players. More participants, more revenue — and participants are supplied by the league funnel and the ambassador reach The base. Predictable, and the number the breakeven is measured against
Beverage sales Scales with players and with the court-side crowd (~30% on top of the playing base), across a three-day event The margin. Drinks carry the gross margin; the cart is the highest-margin surface the Company owns
The cart, not the café Growth comes from more events and more carts — not from leases and build-outs This is the core structural bet. Capacity is bought per event, not carried as fixed cost. It is why the model expands without real estate

Read this line honestly

In the POC, beverage is a proof line, not a profit line — and the Company would rather say so than dress it up. Entry fees carry the POC's economics because a first-year event business is priced to fill. What the drink must prove in this phase is the unit economics that make the scaled model work: that players buy at the price point, that the margin holds, and that a cart can serve the volume in the rhythm of play. Those three facts are what the Phase 2 build rests on. A beverage brand that cannot yet show a large beverage profit line, but can show a validated per-player drink rate and a proven margin, is exactly where a well-run POC should be.

What the drink must proveWhy it decides the scaled model
Drinks per player
—		The single most important number in the beverage line. It is the multiplier on every future event, every cart, and every venue. Get it right at three tournaments and it can be projected with confidence across thirty
Price tolerance
—		Whether a court-sport community pays a specialty price for a specialty drink — the premise of the entire brand. If it holds at a tournament, it holds at a club
Gross margin
—		A non-exclusive roaster supply model must deliver a margin that survives cart, staff, and event costs. This is the number that determines whether more carts mean more profit — or just more work
Cart throughput
Serve rate in the gap between games		The operational constraint. A player will not wait ten minutes when their game starts in five — so throughput, not just demand, caps beverage revenue per event

Where beverage goes after the POC. Volume is a function of touchpoints, and the POC deliberately buys only three. The scaled model adds permanent cart placements at partner venues — where the drink is sold every day the courts are open, not three days a quarter. That is the transition from a beverage line that proves a margin to one that carries the business, and it is what the warrant funds.

The app — a deferred revenue engine

The app earns nothing in the POC, and that is deliberate. Its job in this phase is to assemble and own the community: registration, game organisation, league management, and the rewards layer — moving the relationship off third-party group chats and onto a channel the Company controls. Monetisation is a Phase 2 decision, and the thesis is a player subscription.

The subscription thesis — illustrative, not in the POC

Players already pay to play. What they do not yet have is a tool that reliably gets them into games — organising a four, finding a court, running a league, tracking a ladder. The Company's view is that once the app becomes the way a player secures consistent play, a subscription in the region of $25 per month is defensible — because the alternative is not another app, it is the WhatsApp group and the guesswork it replaces. Venues are not the payer; the player is.

Why this line matters more than its sizeExplanation
It is recurringTournaments are episodic and lumpy — revenue arrives three times a year and requires an operation each time. A subscription arrives every month and requires nothing incremental
It is high marginNo carts, no staff, no venue, no COGS. Beyond hosting and development, the marginal cost of the next subscriber is close to zero — against roughly 50% gross on a drink and a labour-intensive event

How the parts feed each other
  1. The league acquires and retains — cheap, recurring, community-building. Near-zero net cost.
  2. The app owns the relationship — registration, organisation, and rewards move off third-party group chats onto a channel the Company controls. Not a revenue line in the POC; a moat.
  3. The ambassadors extend reach — trusted voices inside the community, not paid media. They convert their own networks and they recruit the next operators.
  4. The tournament monetises — entry fees plus beverage, at a margin that funds everything above it.
  5. The brand compounds — each event makes the next one easier to fill, and cheaper to fill.

The POC exists to prove one loop: that leagues fill tournaments, that tournaments pay for themselves and for the leagues, and that the whole thing runs on carts rather than leases. Everything in Tab 8 is the arithmetic of that loop.
